\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

We study parameter inference in a stochastic SIR epidemic model on an adaptive network \cite{GDB06}, where susceptible nodes can rewire away from infected contacts at rate $\rho$. The network begins as an Erdős–Rényi random graph $G(200, 0.05)$, yielding expected degree $\approx 10$ and connectivity with high probability \cite{Wik24}. The likelihood is intractable because the network co-evolves with the epidemic, so we apply Approximate Bayesian Computation (ABC). Three parameters are inferred from uniform priors:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{clc}
\toprule
\textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Meaning} & \textbf{Prior} \\
\midrule
$\beta$ & Infection probability per S--I edge per step & $\mathrm{Uniform}(0.05,\,0.50)$ \\
$\gamma$ & Recovery probability per infected node per step & $\mathrm{Uniform}(0.02,\,0.20)$ \\
$\rho$ & Rewiring probability per S--I edge per step & $\mathrm{Uniform}(0.0,\,0.8)$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Model parameters and priors.}
\end{table}

\noindent The epidemic peaks around $t \approx 10$--$13$ (infected fraction 0.6--0.8) and decays to near-zero by $t=200$. Each time step applies three sequential phases: Infection $\to$ Recovery $\to$ Rewiring. The observed dataset contains 40 replicate trajectories of infected fraction, rewiring counts, and the final degree histogram. All code and analysis scripts are available at: \url{https://github.com/makspacz12/Simulation_project}.



% ============================================================
\section{Rejection ABC}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Setup}

We run 10,000 prior simulations of the adaptive-network SIR model \cite{GDB06} ($N=200$, $p=0.05$, 5 initial infecteds, $T=200$ steps). For each simulation $i$, we compute a vector of summary statistics $\mathbf{s}_i$ and accept the parameter triple if the normalised Euclidean distance
\[
d_i = \left\| \frac{\mathbf{s}_i - \mathbf{s}^{\mathrm{obs}}}{\boldsymbol{\sigma}} \right\|_2
\]
falls within the $\varepsilon$-quantile of all distances ($\sigma_j$ = prior predictive std of statistic $j$). We evaluate $\varepsilon \in \{1\%, 5\%, 10\%, 20\%\}$; the primary criterion is $\varepsilon=5\%$ ($n=500$) \cite{PSP99}.

\subsection{Evaluation of Parameter Inference}
\label{sec:rejection}

Inference quality is assessed by three diagnostics: (i)~\textbf{marginal shrinkage} — how much the posterior contracts relative to the prior; (ii)~\textbf{tolerance robustness} — whether posteriors are stable across $\varepsilon$ levels; (iii)~\textbf{joint identifiability} — whether the $\beta$--$\rho$ posterior correlation $|r|$ remains below 0.3 (our identifiability threshold).



% ============================================================
\section{Summary Statistics}
\label{sec:stats}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Baseline and Informed Designs}

The baseline design uses three temporal means $(\bar I,\bar R,\bar k)$, which average over the full epidemic and discard the time ordering of infection, recovery, and rewiring. Empirically, this design is weak for inference: it produces only limited contraction for $\beta$ and leaves a strong residual $\beta$--$\rho$ confound. This is expected, since both lower transmission and stronger rewiring reduce epidemic intensity when viewed only through global averages.

The informed design is constructed to exploit the sequential phase ordering \cite{GDB06} of the model. At $t=1$, infection has acted once but rewiring has not yet occurred, so the first-step infection increase gives a near-pure signal for $\beta$. Over the first few time steps, rewiring-per-infected-node provides a near-pure signal for $\rho$. Post-peak decline summaries isolate $\gamma$, while time-specific rewiring counts add compound information that helps break the remaining $\beta$--$\rho$ tradeoff. The resulting 8-statistic set is therefore evaluated not just as a larger collection of summaries, but as a deliberately complementary set of pure, semi-pure, and compound probes.

\subsection{Progressive Comparison}

Table~\ref{tab:setcomp} compares four summary-statistic designs at $\varepsilon=5\%$: naive, baseline, partial mechanistic, and full informed. We assess each set using two criteria: identifiability, measured by $|r(\beta,\rho)|$, and informativeness, measured by posterior shrinkage.

The naive and baseline sets rely mainly on aggregated epidemic summaries and therefore fail to separate transmission from rewiring. Both leave strong residual $\beta$--$\rho$ confounding, with the baseline design performing worst overall. The partial mechanistic set shows that adding a small number of targeted summaries already improves inference substantially: with one broad epidemic statistic and near-pure signals for $\beta$ and $\rho$, it reduces the confound to below the identification threshold. However, it still provides uneven contraction across parameters, especially for $\gamma$.

The full informed set performs best because it combines complementary summaries that target distinct parts of the model dynamics. Early-time statistics identify $\beta$ and $\rho$, post-peak decay statistics identify $\gamma$, and compound rewiring summaries reduce the remaining $\beta$--$\rho$ tradeoff. It is therefore the only design that achieves both low $|r(\beta,\rho)|$ and substantial shrinkage for all three parameters, showing that summary quality depends more on signal specificity and complementarity than on the number of statistics alone.

\section{Advanced Methods}
% ============================================================

\subsection{ABC-MCMC}

ABC-MCMC \cite{Mar03} extends rejection ABC by exploring parameter space via a Markov chain rather than independent prior samples. At each iteration, a proposal
\[
\theta' = \theta_t + \eta, \quad \eta \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \Sigma),
\]
is simulated and accepted if $d(\theta') \le \varepsilon$; otherwise the chain stays at $\theta_t$. Because proposals stay near accepted regions, the chain can target a \emph{tighter} $\varepsilon$ than rejection ABC without additional prior sampling. Matching the observation model requires simulating multiple replicates per proposal and aggregating them the same way as the observed data, so each MCMC step is substantially more expensive than a single prior-bank draw.

We implement ABC-MCMC with the informed 8-statistic design at $\varepsilon=2\%$ ($\varepsilon_{2\%}=1.029$), calibrated on a bank of 100 prior draws where each draw is aggregated over 10 simulated replicates (matching the aggregation used for the observed summary). Each proposal is evaluated by simulating 10 replicates and aggregating them the same way as the 40-replicate observed dataset; this is a reduced-replicate approximation of the full 40-replicate observation model, adopted to keep total simulation cost comparable to the 10,000-draw rejection ABC and regression-adjustment pipelines. The chain starts at the rejection ABC medians ($\hat\beta=0.179$, $\hat\gamma=0.088$, $\hat\rho=0.297$), uses an adaptive Gaussian random walk, and runs for 2,000 iterations with 500 burn-in, leaving 1,500 post-burn-in draws. Because the priors are uniform on bounded intervals and the proposal is symmetric, the ABC-MCMC acceptance rule reduces to accepting in-support proposals with $d(\theta') \le \varepsilon$ and otherwise remaining at $\theta_t$. Post-burn-in acceptance rate: 42.7\%, slightly above the conventional 15--40\% band; the adaptive $\Sigma$ could be tightened further, but the chain mixes well over the burn-in and post-burn-in traces.

The positive $\beta$--$\rho$ relationship seen in preliminary ABC-MCMC analysis is precisely the confound identified in Section~\ref{sec:stats}. With informed statistics this is resolved: the chain explores a near-orthogonal $\beta$-$\rho$ posterior under the informed statistics.

\textbf{Results.} Relative to rejection ABC, ABC-MCMC yields a substantially more concentrated posterior under the same informed 8-statistic design. The posterior centres remain very similar, but the spread is reduced for all three parameters (Table~\ref{tab:mcmc}), indicating that the main effect of the Markov chain is to refine the posterior rather than to change the qualitative inference. The gain is strongest for $\beta$ and $\rho$: shrinkage increases from $0.522$ to $0.729$ for $\beta$ and from $0.599$ to $0.708$ for $\rho$, while the 95\% credible intervals narrow from $[0.092,0.327]$ to $[0.103,0.236]$ and from $[0.157,0.498]$ to $[0.175,0.420]$, respectively. For $\gamma$, the improvement is more modest, with shrinkage increasing from $0.505$ to $0.564$ and the posterior median moving only slightly from $0.088$ to $0.083$. Figure~\ref{fig:mcmc_post} shows this visually: ABC-MCMC mainly contracts the marginal posteriors without materially shifting their location. Figure~\ref{fig:mcmc_ppc} further shows that posterior predictive simulations from ABC-MCMC reproduce the observed infected-fraction trajectory, rewiring trajectory, and final degree histogram well, with the 95\% credible bands covering the observed mean patterns.

\subsection{Regression Adjustment}

Regression adjustment \cite{BZB02} corrects for the residual bias caused by accepting simulations that are only approximately close to the observed data. For each accepted sample, we regress each parameter on the summary-statistic discrepancy $\delta_i = \mathbf{s}_i - \mathbf{s}^{\mathrm{obs}}$ and then project to the idealised case $\delta = 0$, giving the adjusted value
\[
\theta_i^{*} = \theta_i - \hat{\beta}_{\mathrm{reg}}^{\top}\delta_i .
\]
This yields a sharper posterior approximation without requiring a smaller tolerance $\varepsilon$.

\textbf{Results.} Applied to the informed statistics at $\varepsilon=10\%$ ($n=1{,}000$), regression adjustment narrows the posterior for all three parameters relative to the unadjusted rejection ABC posterior even at $\varepsilon=5\%$ (Figure~\ref{fig:regadj_post}). Posterior medians also remain stable across tolerance levels from $1\%$ to $20\%$ (Table~\ref{tab:regadj_stability}), with $\hat\beta \in [0.166, 0.183]$, $\hat\gamma \in [0.086, 0.090]$, and $\hat\rho \in [0.314, 0.320]$, indicating low sensitivity to the acceptance threshold. The adjusted posterior predictive distribution (Figure~\ref{fig:regadj_trajectory}) reproduces the observed infection trajectory, rewiring dynamics, and final degree histogram well. Figure~\ref{fig:degree_ppc} gives the corresponding 3-panel posterior predictive check at the rejection ABC posterior, confirming good structural fit under both inference methods.

Compared with ABC-MCMC (Table~\ref{tab:reg_adj}), regression adjustment gives a more concentrated posterior for $\gamma$ (shrinkage $0.747$ vs $0.564$) and $\rho$ ($0.835$ vs $0.708$). For $\beta$, ABC-MCMC achieves slightly higher shrinkage than regression adjustment ($0.729$ vs $0.662$), reflecting that the Markov chain concentrates draws in the high-posterior region of $\beta$ once the chain has adapted. The posterior centres under the two methods remain similar overall, so the main effect of regression adjustment is to reduce posterior spread rather than to change the qualitative inference.

\section{Method Comparison and Final Estimates}
% ============================================================

All three inference methods use the same informed 8-statistic design. Rejection ABC and regression adjustment are built from the same 10,000-simulation prior bank, while ABC-MCMC uses the same summaries but refines inference through a separate Markov chain targeting a tighter tolerance. ABC-MCMC ran for 2,000 iterations with 10 replicates simulated per proposal, so that the total number of simulator calls is of the same order as the 10,000-draw rejection ABC and regression-adjustment pipelines; full 40-replicate aggregation per proposal would have been an order of magnitude slower than the other two methods under our compute budget. Table~\ref{tab:comparison} presents a unified comparison: posterior medians, 95\% credible intervals, and shrinkage relative to the prior.

The three inference methods lead to broadly consistent posterior centres, which supports the robustness of the main conclusions, but they differ in posterior sharpness. Rejection ABC with informed statistics already resolves the main $\beta$--$\rho$ confound and gives a well-calibrated baseline. ABC-MCMC produces a more concentrated posterior than rejection ABC, especially for $\beta$ and $\rho$, because the Markov chain spends more computation in high-posterior regions rather than drawing repeatedly from the full prior. Regression-adjusted ABC gives the sharpest posterior on average, winning on $\gamma$ and $\rho$ while remaining close to ABC-MCMC on $\beta$, and it preserves good posterior predictive fit to the infected trajectory, rewiring trajectory, and final degree histogram. This is plausible because the informed summaries are already highly structured, so the local regression correction can remove residual mismatch within the accepted sample efficiently. Since regression adjustment yields the highest shrinkage while remaining stable across tolerance levels and consistent with the other two methods, we take it as our final inferential estimate. We therefore take the regression-adjusted estimates as final: $\hat\beta = 0.176\ [0.109, 0.284]$, $\hat\gamma = 0.089\ [0.066, 0.119]$, $\hat\rho = 0.316\ [0.244, 0.393]$, where brackets denote 95\% credible intervals.

As a final validation check, we performed a synthetic-truth recovery experiment by selecting one simulated dataset as pseudo-observed data and rerunning inference while treating its generating parameters as unknown. Figure~\ref{fig:reg_adj_truth} regression-adjusted ABC on this test case, as compared to rejection ABC. For all three parameters, the true values lie within the recovered 95\% credible intervals under both methods, indicating that the rejection-based pipeline is not systematically biased on this example. The adjusted posterior is visibly more concentrated, especially for $\gamma$ and $\rho$, while still retaining coverage of the truth. This supports the validity of the overall inference pipeline and suggests that regression adjustment improves posterior efficiency without sacrificing calibration.

\section{Conclusion}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Parameter-level findings}
$\rho$ is identified most cleanly (shrinkage 0.599 by rejection ABC, 0.835 by regression adjustment), driven by the early rewire-per-infected ratio s5 ($r(\rho)=0.945$). $\beta$ is only identifiable after breaking the $\beta$--$\rho$ confound via the first-step statistic s2 ($r(\rho)=0.003$); without it, the posterior is near-prior-flat. $\gamma$ is moderately identified (shrinkage 0.505--0.747) through post-peak decay statistics s3/s4, independently of the other two. Best estimates from regression-adjusted ABC ($\varepsilon=10\%$, see Table~\ref{tab:comparison}): $\hat\beta=0.176$ [0.109, 0.284], $\hat\gamma=0.089$ [0.066, 0.119], $\hat\rho=0.316$ [0.244, 0.393]. Residual uncertainty in $\beta$ (CI width $\approx 0.18$) reflects the inherent difficulty of separating transmission from rewiring; additional early-time or pre-epidemic network observations would reduce it further.

\paragraph{Robustness and validity}
Four independent checks support the conclusions. (1)~\emph{Synthetic truth recovery}: all 95\% CIs cover the true values, confirming no systematic bias. (2)~\emph{Posterior predictive checks}: the posterior reproduces infected fraction, rewiring, and degree distributions well for all three inference methods. (3)~\emph{Regression adjustment} converges to the same posterior centre as rejection ABC (within 0.02 per parameter) with smaller spread, confirming tolerance robustness. (4)~\emph{ABC-MCMC} at the tighter $\varepsilon=2\%$ independently produces consistent point estimates, cross-validating the posterior location across two methodologically distinct algorithms. Remaining uncertainty is likely irreducible at this sample size; semi-automatic statistic selection \cite{FP12} could yield further gains.

\paragraph{Transparency}
AI tools (Claude, ChatGPT, and Copilot) were used to assist with code drafting, figure generation, wording refinement, and LaTex structuring, but all final numerical results, plots, and interpretations were verified independently by the authors. Several alternatives were explored and discarded during development. First, degree variance was tested as a candidate $\gamma$ statistic, but it was dropped because it loaded primarily on $\rho$, reflecting the accumulation of isolated nodes under high rewiring rather than recovery dynamics. Second, a naive two-statistic design was rejected early because it showed strong residual confounding, with $|r(\beta,\rho)| = 0.599$. Third, in regions of the prior with very small epidemics (roughly $\beta < 0.08$ and $\rho > 0.7$), the log-decline statistic $s_3$ became numerically unstable as the infected fraction approached zero; this was handled by clamping the infected fraction away from zero before taking logs.
